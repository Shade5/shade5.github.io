Studying the interaction of particles at the solid-liquid interface has been a difficult process as the R-Y Theory that describes this process requires the two-point correlation function which is extremely difficult to obtain via experiments. As an alternative, it can be calculated using computational techniques like Metropolis simulations.
In this project a system of particles under the influence of a substrate potential is simulated as follows:

  • Creating substrate potential mesh
  • A random distribution of particles, sampled using Metropolis simulations
  • Rerunning the simulation for various temperatures to obtain solid-liquid interface data
  • Two-point correlation function is plugged into R-Y Theory

The GIF above shows the distribution of particles at different temperatures. Chaos dies with temperature loss.

The substrate particles were placed in an ordered grid, fixed at their initial location. The liquid particles were free to move.
The substrate potential was precalculated as it will always remain constant.
At low temperatures, the particles are likely to be present at the center of four substrate particles, the most stable location.
The Correlation Function for different values of temperature are plotted here. Particles are evenly distributed at high temperatures.
